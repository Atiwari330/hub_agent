Opus Vendor Coordination Policy v1
Purpose
This policy defines how Opus Support should manage issues that involve third-party vendors, white-labeled systems, integrated systems, and external service providers.
The purpose of this policy is to:
ensure customers experience Opus as the single support front door
prevent customers from being pushed between Opus and vendors
create consistency in how vendor-dependent issues are handled
clarify when and how Tier 1 Support should engage vendors
define ownership, communication expectations, and escalation behavior
ensure vendor dependency does not lead to passive support behavior
This policy should be used together with the Opus Support Authority Matrix, Customer Authorization and Approval Policy, and Severity and Escalation Policy.

Core Policy Principle
Opus remains the customer-facing owner of support, even when resolution depends on a vendor.
Customers should not be expected to coordinate directly with third-party vendors for standard support matters.
When a vendor is involved, Opus Support still owns:
triage
case management
follow-up
communication
expectation-setting
escalation
advocacy on the customer’s behalf
Vendor dependency does not reduce Opus’s responsibility to manage the case professionally.

Default Support Philosophy
Tier 1 Support should be:
proactive
persistent
organized
accountable
communicative
customer-advocacy driven
Support should never take the posture of:
“this is the vendor’s problem”
“we are waiting and there is nothing else to do”
“the customer should contact the vendor”
“we opened a vendor case, so our part is done”
That is not the Opus support model.
The correct posture is:
“We are working this on our side, coordinating with the appropriate partner as needed, and we will continue to keep you updated.”

Scope of This Policy
This policy applies whenever a customer issue involves:
a white-labeled platform
a third-party integration
a connected external service
a vendor-owned backend dependency
a vendor-controlled access/settings workflow
a vendor-side outage, degradation, or malfunction
an external system that Opus sells, supports, or presents as part of the Opus ecosystem
Examples may include, but are not limited to:
RCM platform issues
clearinghouse-dependent issues
payment-processor-related issues
CRM vendor-related issues
ePrescribe vendor-related issues
vendor-connected SSO or access issues
integration behavior involving vendor-controlled systems

Single Front Door Rule
For standard support matters, the customer should come to Opus Support, not to the vendor.
Opus Support should then determine:
whether the issue is vendor-dependent
whether internal troubleshooting should happen first
whether vendor engagement is necessary
what information needs to be collected before vendor engagement
how the issue should be communicated back to the customer
Support should not tell the customer to “go open a case with the vendor” as a standard practice.

Case Ownership Rule
Even when a vendor is involved, the Opus support case remains owned by Opus.
This means Tier 1 Support is responsible for:
documenting the issue internally
gathering the needed details
opening or progressing the vendor case when appropriate
tracking status
driving follow-up
updating the customer
escalating internally if vendor response is too slow or insufficient
The vendor may own the root cause or technical remediation, but Opus owns the customer experience.

When Vendor Engagement Is Appropriate
Vendor engagement is appropriate when:
initial troubleshooting suggests the issue depends on a vendor-controlled system
the issue involves a known vendor-supported function
vendor-side action is required for investigation
vendor-side action is required for remediation
a vendor-owned access/settings layer is implicated
internal Opus teams cannot fully resolve the issue without vendor involvement
the issue appears to be caused by a vendor outage, defect, or service disruption
Tier 1 should not engage a vendor reflexively before doing reasonable internal triage.
Support should first determine whether the issue may actually be:
user error
customer configuration issue
training gap
approval issue
internal Opus issue
integration logic issue requiring internal engineering review first

Required Internal Triage Before Vendor Engagement
Before contacting a vendor, Tier 1 should complete reasonable first-level triage whenever practical.
This may include:
gathering a clear description of the issue
identifying affected users, accounts, or tenants
determining whether the issue is isolated or broad
confirming timestamps and recent changes
reproducing the problem if possible
checking whether the issue is actually access-related, training-related, or configuration-related
performing standard troubleshooting steps
documenting business impact
determining whether the issue is urgent
The goal is to avoid weak vendor escalations that waste time and delay resolution.

Information Tier 1 Should Gather Before Contacting a Vendor
Before opening or progressing a vendor case, Tier 1 should gather as much of the following as possible:
customer name
account or tenant name
affected user(s)
exact issue description
date and time of occurrence
screenshots or error messages when available
steps to reproduce
whether issue is ongoing or intermittent
severity/business impact
whether a workaround exists
what troubleshooting has already been completed
whether engineering has already reviewed the issue internally
whether the issue affects one customer or multiple customers
Not every item will always be available, but vendor outreach should be strong enough to move the case forward efficiently.

Vendor Communication Standard
When contacting vendors, Tier 1 should communicate clearly, concisely, and professionally.
Vendor communication should include:
what the issue is
who is affected
what has already been tried
what the current impact is
what Opus needs from the vendor
how urgent the issue is
Vendor communication should be documented internally so the support record shows:
when vendor contact occurred
what was shared
what response was received
what the next expected step is

Customer Communication Standard During Vendor Issues
Customers should not be left uncertain simply because a vendor is involved.
If a case is pending vendor response or vendor action, Tier 1 should continue to update the customer proactively.
Default Rule
If the issue remains open, the customer should receive updates at least daily, unless a different expectation has been explicitly set.
Support should continue to communicate even when the update is simply:
vendor case has been opened
follow-up has been sent
investigation is still in progress
internal escalation is underway
next expected checkpoint is pending
Customers should not feel abandoned while Opus waits on a vendor.

Approved Customer-Facing Language
Support should speak in a way that preserves ownership.
Preferred phrasing:
“We are actively working this on our side and coordinating with the appropriate partner.”
“We’ve escalated this through the appropriate channels and will continue to keep you updated.”
“Our team is still actively following up on this and we’ll continue to push it forward.”
Avoid phrasing such as:
“This is the vendor’s issue.”
“You need to contact the vendor.”
“We can’t do anything until they respond.”
“That system is not really ours.”
The customer bought the Opus experience, not a list of excuses.

Vendor Response Management
Support should not become passive after opening a vendor case.
Tier 1 is expected to:
follow up on open vendor cases
push for updates when timelines are unclear
clarify vague vendor responses
request ETA or next-step clarity when appropriate
escalate internally if vendor follow-up is not progressing
continue to advocate for the customer until resolution or a clearly communicated path forward exists
Opening a vendor case is the beginning of vendor coordination, not the end of it.

When to Escalate Vendor Issues Internally
Vendor-dependent issues should be escalated internally when:
the issue is Severity 1
the vendor is slow to respond
vendor responses are unclear or insufficient
the issue is affecting multiple customers
the issue is affecting a key account
business impact is rising
the vendor appears to be blocking progress
the rep is not getting traction
the issue needs management help to move forward
the issue may require engineering-to-vendor communication
When progress stalls, the Customer Support Manager should get involved and help drive resolution.

Vendor Issues and Severity
Vendor involvement does not reduce severity.
If a vendor-dependent issue is materially blocking claims transmission, prescribing, broad access, or other mission-critical workflows, it should still be treated according to the Severity and Escalation Policy.
In other words:
vendor issue does not mean non-urgent
vendor issue does not mean “wait and see”
vendor issue does not mean customer updates can slow down
Severity should still be determined by real business impact.

Vendor Issues Involving Multiple Customers
When a vendor-related issue appears to affect multiple accounts, tenants, or workflows, Support should treat that as a broader operational concern.
In those situations, Support should:
document the pattern clearly
determine whether there is a common dependency
escalate internally with urgency if warranted
coordinate with engineering or management as needed
avoid treating each ticket as if it were unrelated when a broader issue may exist
Broader patterns should be surfaced quickly.

Vendor-Dependent Access and Settings Changes
Some vendor-connected functions involve access, permissions, or settings that Tier 1 cannot directly change.
In those situations, Tier 1 should:
determine whether the issue is informational, configuration-related, or access-related
confirm whether customer authorization is required
route the matter to the correct internal owner if direct Tier 1 action is not allowed
engage the vendor only when appropriate
continue owning communication with the customer
Vendor dependency does not give Tier 1 permission to bypass internal authority rules.

Documentation Standard for Vendor Cases
Whenever a vendor is involved, the ticket should clearly reflect:
why vendor involvement was needed
what internal triage was completed first
when the vendor was contacted
what information was sent
what the vendor responded
whether follow-up is pending
whether internal escalation has occurred
when the customer was updated
what the next step is
Good vendor coordination requires visible case history.

Behaviors to Avoid
Support should avoid:
sending the customer to the vendor
contacting the vendor without sufficient triage
opening weak or vague vendor cases
failing to document vendor communication
assuming vendor delay is acceptable
going silent while waiting on vendor action
using vendor dependency as a reason to lower urgency
failing to escalate vendor issues internally when they stall
treating vendor-managed systems as outside the Opus customer experience

Practical Examples
Example 1: ePrescribe Workflow Is Failing
Tier 1 should perform first-level troubleshooting, gather details, determine business impact, and escalate internally or to the vendor as appropriate. Opus remains the customer-facing owner throughout.
Example 2: Claims Are Not Transmitting Due to Clearinghouse-Dependent Behavior
Support should treat this based on actual impact. If claims cannot be transmitted, the issue is urgent regardless of vendor dependency. Vendor coordination should begin quickly, but Opus still owns communication and escalation.
Example 3: CRM-Related Issue Requires Vendor Investigation
Tier 1 should gather issue details, document internal triage, contact the vendor appropriately, and continue updating the customer while the case progresses.
Example 4: Vendor Has Not Responded for Too Long
Tier 1 should follow up, escalate internally to the Customer Support Manager, continue customer updates, and avoid passive waiting.
Example 5: Customer Asks for Vendor Contact Information So They Can Handle It Themselves
Tier 1 should reinforce that Opus is managing the issue and will continue coordinating on the customer’s behalf, unless there is a specific approved exception.

Exception Handling
Exceptions to the single-front-door model should be rare.
If there is ever a reason the customer must interact directly with a vendor, that should be a deliberate exception, not the default process.
Any such exception should be reviewed internally before being communicated.

Final Operating Statement
Customers should experience Opus as the owner of support, even when vendors are involved behind the scenes.
Tier 1 Support is expected to triage well, coordinate actively, push vendors persistently, communicate clearly, and advocate for the customer until the issue is resolved or a well-defined path forward exists.
Vendor dependency is not an excuse for weak ownership.
